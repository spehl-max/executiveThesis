Radiochemistry

Because radioisotopes decay at a known rate, by comparing the activity of a radionuclide at one point in a distribution system to a point downstream, the travel time of water between the two locations may be calculated. Water containing both 90Y and 90Sr enters the treatment plant from Lake Michigan. During the coagulation step of the treatment process, all the particle-bound 90Y was assumed, and later confirmed to be removed from the flowing water, leaving only its parent, 90Sr. As 90Sr flows through the pipes, it decays into 90Y at a rate determined by its half-life. Therefore, by measuring the activity of 90Y in a sample of drinking water, the water age can be calculated using the half-life of 90Y.

Below, an example decay graph is shown. 90Y is clearly decaying exponentially, occurring to its half-life, 64.1 hrs.

Fortunately, the influence of the endogenously produced 90Y on the measured activity dissipated at the most distal stations, four and five. Therefore, water age calculations were only meaningful for samples collected at these two stations. 

water_age_plot_df <- Meta_Data %>% select(sample_name_abridged, Station_Num, Collection_Date) %>% left_join(age_and_activity, by = c("sample_name_abridged" = "sample_name")) %>% filter(Station_Num %in% c("4", "5")) %>% mutate(Station_Num = fct_relevel(Station_Num, "4", "5")) %>% filter(!is.na(water_age_hrs))

The water age at station 4 was 48.21 ± 25.30 hours (n = 6, ± 1 SD) and the water age at station 5 was 76.74 ± 14.21 hours (n = 5, ±1 SD). As hypothesized, the water age increased distally from the plant. Water age also increased seasonally, suggesting decreasing water usage during the cooler months; most likely because of a reduction in sprinkler usage.
